# Frameworks — archetypes and the evaluation rubrics

The named frameworks the studio uses: archetypes drive **generation** (sound + name-type choices);
Neumeier and SMILE/SCRATCH drive **evaluation** (the scored challenge in
[`../../skills/name-search/references/evaluation-rubric.md`](../../skills/name-search/references/evaluation-rubric.md)).

## Jung's 12 brand archetypes → naming cues

Pick **1–2** in discovery (a 70/30 core+influencer split). Each implies a sound palette
([`phonosemantics.md`](phonosemantics.md)) and favoured name-types ([`name-types.md`](name-types.md)).

| Archetype | Drive | Naming cues |
|---|---|---|
| **Innocent** | safety, optimism, purity | soft consonants, *-y/-ie* diminutives, bright words |
| **Explorer** | freedom, discovery | action verbs, plosives (k, g), geographic/wild references |
| **Sage** | truth, wisdom | Latin/Greek roots, knowledge metaphors (TED, Wolfram) |
| **Hero** | courage, mastery, victory | strong sounds, victory/power refs, action (Nike, Gatorade) |
| **Outlaw** | disruption, rebellion | reclaimed/edgy words, unconventional spelling |
| **Magician** | transformation, vision | invented words & mysterious phonetics, scientific allusion (Sony, Häagen-Dazs) |
| **Lover** | intimacy, passion | soft m/n/l, sensory words, romantic/mythic allusion (Pandora) |
| **Jester** | humour, play | puns, reduplication, slang, the comedic "k" |
| **Everyman** | belonging, simplicity | short everyday words, friendly diminutives (Ford, IKEA) |
| **Caregiver** | service, nurture | warm vowels, soft consonants, care language (Nestlé) |
| **Ruler** | control, order, prestige | formal Latin/Greek, structured compounds (BMW, Microsoft) |
| **Creator** | innovation, expression | portmanteaus, playful blends, future-language (Verizon, Lego) |

## Marty Neumeier's 7 criteria (from *The Brand Gap* / *Zag*)

The evaluation backbone — score each 1–5:

1. **Distinctiveness** — stands out in a sentence/crowded room (plosive initials excel).
2. **Brevity** — ≤4 syllables, ideally 1–2 (or users abbreviate it).
3. **Appropriateness** — fits the business without being literal; works for future extensions too.
4. **Easy spelling & pronunciation** — most people spell it right after hearing it once.
5. **Likability / mouth-feel** — pleasant to say; would you enjoy saying it 100×?
6. **Extendability / "legs"** — stretches across product lines, stories, visuals.
7. **Protectability** — trademarkable; not a sound-alike of an existing mark.

## Alexandra Watkins' SMILE & SCRATCH (from *Hello, My Name Is Awesome*)

A great name makes you **SMILE**:
- **S**uggestive — evokes the brand (not literal).
- **M**emorable — strong familiar association.
- **I**magery — paints a mental picture (aids recall).
- **L**egs — lends itself to extended creative mileage.
- **E**motional — moves people; triggers aspiration.

A bad name makes you **SCRATCH** your head — each is a demerit:
- **S**pelling-challenged — looks like a typo (Flickr, Imgur).
- **C**opycat — too close to a competitor (confusion/legal).
- **R**estrictive — limits future growth/geography.
- **A**nnoying — forced, contrived, frustrating.
- **T**ame — flat, generic, blends in.
- **C**urse-of-knowledge — only insiders get it.
- **H**ard-to-pronounce — distances people, kills word-of-mouth.

## How the frameworks compose

Discovery picks the archetype(s) → generation uses the archetype's sound + name-type cues →
evaluation scores Neumeier 7 + checks SMILE-present and SCRATCH-flags + archetype-fit +
sound-symbolism-fit. A name can be *available* (registries clean) yet still **fail the challenge** on
these axes — the two verdicts stay in separate columns (the report's cardinal rule).
